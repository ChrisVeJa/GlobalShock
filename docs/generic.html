Introduction

Tipically, terms of trade (approximated by ratio of export price index and the import price index) have been considered as the main drivers behind commodity-exporting emerging countries dynamic. This is at fact a well-known assumption for LATAM countries. Fro example, based on a calibrated DSGE model Mendoza (1995), concluded that commodity fluctuations explain about 30 percent of domestic GDP variability. In addition, a recent document by Zeev,Pappa, and Vicondoa (2017, henceforth ZPV) using information for commodity-exporting countries (Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Colombia, and Peru), and using medium-run identification (or news shock identification) showed that news-augmented shocks related to commodity-based terms of trade account for 49 percent of domestic GDP fluctuations in the selected countries. In contrast, Schmitt - Grohe and Uribe (2017, henceforth SGU) with annual information of 38 emerging countries found that commodities shocks just explain 10 percent of fluctuations in domestic variables. If the model does not include real effective exchange rate its participation even decreased to 2 percent. According to ZPV those differences are caused by the more information that agents have when the implicit economy allows them move from an straight backward looking behavior to a forward lookine one. Actually news shocks can be seem as any anticipated information that modify your current decisions. However, is it a comprehensive response? are these differences caused strictly by terms of trade news shocks? are question which needs to solve.

In order to clarify it, we need to mention two caveats. First, the above described models lack of a well defined external block which should (i) be exogenous and (ii) represent a global economy, that means we need to include a proxy of global economic activity in addition to commodities prices and monetary conditions variables. Intuitively, a global economy that does not satisfy (i) could create a artificial stronger effect of the medium run identification regards a Cholesky scheme, since commodities prices can be explained by spurious drivers (domestic variables), reducing its own prediction power and (in consequence) its forecast variance decomposition share over the rest of the system. Meanwhile, models that ignore (ii) omit the pricing process of commodities considering them as exogenous variables. In relation with this point, if we include some index of world production or other global real-variable, we can enrich the understanding about our models and overcome some possible external validation. For example, its clear that the stellar growth of China's GDP have influenced the increase of copper price in recent years or that the financial crisis affected commodities prices, properly, a model which does not include these event misses explanation power. The second caveat is deeper, using medium run strategy and calling the estimated innovation as \textit{news-augmented term of trade} shocks have several profound connotations. On the one hand, medium run strategy is a way to find a \textit{structural} shock that explain as high as possible fluctuations over a specific variable $x$, at the moment when we relate this identified shock only with $x$ we are trying to become $x$ into a exogenous variable -but not completely. On the second hand, calling these shocks as ``news" shock of $x$ causes an misapprehension since people could thinks that these news are only for the variable $x$, in this sense is not possible to track the origin of agents expectation changes in our models maybe implying and incomplete identification.

In fact, our hypothesis is based on two central assumptions (related with previous ideas): a) there is not a only source for changes in agents beliefs, and b) is unavailable distinct perfectly what percent of the expectation changes correspond to each source. Given these, we can imagine that contemporaneous movements in global variables are correlated and also there is a common global component which synthesize all this information and can be defined as the main driver of global fluctuations.
