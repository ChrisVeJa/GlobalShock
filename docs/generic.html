Introduction

The current literature recognizes the relevance of commodities prices movements for small open economies. The terms of trade (approximated by ratio of export price index and the import price index) have been considered one of the main drivers in the observed growth for countries like Chile, and Peru in recent years (both mining exporters) with a outstanding copper price due China's developing process.

Here talk about some commodity price literature.

However, this result has been questioned by some authors. In example, Schmitt - Grohe and Uribe (2017, henceforth SGU) with annual information about 38 emerging countries found that commodities shocks are responsible for only 10 percent of domestic variables fluctuations, and when the real exchange rate channel is turning off, the impact is reduced to 2 percent. On the other hand, their results have some flags that I comment. First of all, the unique variable considered in the external block was ..., meaning an implicit assumption of commodity price being a sufficient statistic for the external information, it generates two caveats: (i) the volatility of external real variables and financial ones is not tracing directly and many channels are not considered, and (ii) prices could summarize the economic information if there are not frictions in the market, even if we can argue that commodities are trade in markets close to be competitive, the existence of financial instruments related with them gives a play-role to information asymmetries. Therefore, this assumption is not as harmless as we could think.

In a certain way, Zeev,Pappa, and Vicondoa (2017) partially addresses them. They use a set of of 5 LATAM countries and identifies a news-augmented commodity price shock by medium run identification (Uhlig, 2003) estimating a contribution of commodities to domestic variables around 49 percent. They understanding regards why SGU obtained low levels is that commodities have also a financial role and their pricing takes into account future information, then a short run identification neglects in measuring their total impact. Although, they have a valid point, their identification strategy faces some issues.

The most obvious is if the news-augmented commodities shocks has precisely only information regarding movements in prices (we could think if this shocks are itself orthogonal with the not identified shocks in the foreign block). At maximum, it would be a problem of labeling (leading a mixing between channels, mechanisms and source that I talk later), but in order to this to happen, we need to consider commodities prices as sufficient statistics for the external implicit economy.

The last implies a second aspect, which is more theoretical. Lets imagine that effectively commodities price can span all the information of the global economy, if this is the case, an automatic question is what are the authors identifying? By construction, a medium run identification approach will isolate a shock that could explain in maximum the cumulative prediction error variance, as it is the same information that the whole block it would be considered as a global shock. Then, if it would be possible to have a refinement in the identification of this global shock, the results should not show significant differences with the original and it also should be the main determinant for commodity prices variability.

Lets take figure 1, which plots he evolution of the index of commodity prices calculated by the IMF ( put the link) the growth rate of the index for the GDP G20 (right axis), and the BAA spread, to exemplify this point, with an approximated correlation of 73 percent between the output and commodities prices (-46 percent with the BAA spread, and -20 percent between commodities and financial conditions) it looks like that they have a common component (it is in favor to ZPV), but if ZPV just isolates innovations in commodities movements the fall in 2008 due by the financial crisis should not be part of the news-augmented shocks (it was originated outside the commodity market and its information should be incorporated through predicted falls in commodity prices), but after recover this kind of shocks using the same procedure than ZPV, we realized that the identified "news-augmented" shocks (see figure 2) are accounting the Great Recession as a innovation of their type, denoting a problem in the "labeling". However, is it just a naming issue?

This paper will address these concerns in a simple way. As first element of my strategy I will propose a new way to identify Global Shocks which takes into consideration the whole foreign block information as anchor for identification, then I will inspect its prediction power on commodities fluctuations comparing it with the results from an standard news identification model. In section II, I explain what I have called as global-shocks identification, while in next section, I present these results as well its impact in Small Open Economies fluctuations, at this point I need to emphasize that at difference with SGu and ZPV, I use 10 countries divided in two groups: one for emerging countries and another for developed ones, since I want to show the asymmetric effect between these classes. The second step, involves identify a second identification vector related only with commodities prices (being orthogonal respect the first one) and verify which one is the main driver for commodities, It is presented in section IV and V. The rest of section discuss my findings, concludes and show some robustness exercises.
