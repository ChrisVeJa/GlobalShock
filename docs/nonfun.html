Non-Fundamental shocks

In the group of emerging markets the long run contributions of only-related commodity innovations oscillate in the interval [17:24] percent of the domestic economy fluctuations. In all the cases, the pattern is increasing in the whole horizon (see figure bellow), with the lowest instantaneous impact on financial variables - real interest rate and effective exchange rates, where only explain 3 percent in the same period of the shock. This type of innovation have a sizable impact on investment determining close of 16 percent of its variability after 2 years while only 10 percent of the predictability on production and consumption in the same timespan.

In the case of advanced economies, and in contra position with global shocks, the volatility of the prediction errors depends on non-fundamental shocks more for consumption rather than production and investment. This result could be rationalize as an implication of the PIH hypothesis, since advanced economies have more develop markets and given that non-fundamental shock are not as forecastable as global ones, when the first type on innovation hits they can cover (or are already covered) easier, leading small adjustment on their future consumption, what does not occur for the second type of shocks (see figure bellow).

Contribution of Non fundamental commodity-related shocks to FEV

The contribution of non-fundamental shocks as determinant of trade balance fluctuations for emerging economies is opposite to the outcome for developed markets, although in both scenarios non-fundamental commodities-related shocks explains around 21 percent of the trading forecastibility in the long run, for ECXs takes more time to realize. As later will be exposed, this behave is not contra-intuitive in the sense that emerging countries does not make a good work disentangling commodity price movements into their components reacting closely in both cases, while developed markets can anticipated better global shocks. In comparison with the results in emerging countries, we can distinguish two kinds of patterns in the contribution of non-fundamental shocks on DCXs: (i) an almost constant participation on consumption and trade balance (around 23 percent), and (ii) an increasing contribution more evident in exchanges rates that comes from 4 percent in the short run, passing through 13 percent after 2 years, to finish explaining one fifth of its variability.
