Data Description:

Variables and sources

  1. Global GDP: The global GDP is approximated by the G-20 group real output having as source the OCDE database. This series starts at 1998
  2. Real commodity prices: We consider the database from the IMF website which construct country-specific term of trade measured based on exported commodities and weighted by their relevance on net exports (Gruss and Kebhaj 2019). Although, the dataset has several measures of commodity indexes, I choose the fixed-weights and weighted by ratio of exports to total commodity exports since this has two advantages: a) a fixed-weight measure tends less to structural breaks and it can deal with mis-reportings, and b) using commodity export participation as weights allows us to isolate explicitly the effect caused by their movements without relying on any identification assumption and avoiding any explanation power from domestic variables on commodity prices (the weights take into consideration the bilateral trade). Finally, this indexes are deflacted by the import price index of manufactured goods reported by the FED.

    In the following figure, we can see the evolution of the country specific terms of trade growth rates after being standarized. Almost 80 percent of the correlations (on non standarized prices)among them are higher than 0.8 suggesting a common underlying factor.

    Figure: Evolution of standarized commodity growth rates

  3. National Accounts (GDP, consumption, investment, trade commerce) The national accounts were mostly taken from the IFS database in the IMF website except in the following cases:
    1. Colombia: The dataset was obtained from the central bank of Colombia, and concatenating three different tables, since this country presents its information in different year basis
    2. Peru: The data source was the quarterly dataset of national account in its central bank, this data is not seasonally adjusted, hence I applied an standard X13-Arima procedure.
    Argentina dataset was also adjusted by seasonality using X13-Arima, while in order to complete the data for South Africa I used data comes from its central bank.
  4. Effective exchange rate and interest rates
    5. In both cases, the BIS databases were the source.
